What Goal 7 stands for

What Is UN Sustainable Development Goal 7?

Goal 7 is the United Nations commitment to “ensure access to affordable, reliable, sustainable and modern energy for all.” The goal matters because energy underpins education, healthcare, communications, economic development, and quality of life.[1], [2]

SDG 7 is not limited to adding more electricity. It focuses on whether energy is accessible, dependable, cleaner to use, and supported by the infrastructure and international partnerships needed to scale solutions. In practice, that means expanding electricity access, increasing the share of renewable energy, improving energy efficiency, boosting clean-energy research and investment, and upgrading infrastructure in developing regions.[1], [2]

For students, SDG 7 connects global challenges to everyday life: dorm electricity use, campus transit, indoor air quality, utility bills, internship opportunities, and the technologies engineers are building right now.
Official UN targets

The Five SDG 7 Targets

These target statements are adapted from the official United Nations SDG 7 target framework.[1]

Target 7.1

Universal Modern Energy Access

By 2030, ensure universal access to affordable, reliable, and modern energy services.[1]

Target 7.2

Increase Renewables

Substantially increase the share of renewable energy in the global energy mix by 2030.[1]

Target 7.3

Improve Efficiency

Double the global rate of improvement in energy efficiency by 2030.[1]

Target 7.a

Expand Research and Investment

Enhance international cooperation on clean energy research, technology, and investment.[1]

Target 7.b

Build Infrastructure

Expand and upgrade energy infrastructure and technology for developing countries.[1]

UN Progress Snapshot

What the 2022 UN Goal 7 Report Highlights

The United Nations’ 2022 Goal 7 report shows that progress continues, but not fast enough to meet 2030 targets. Electricity access has improved, yet hundreds of millions remain unserved, clean cooking access is still far behind, renewable deployment needs to accelerate, and energy-efficiency gains remain below the pace required.[32]

Electricity Access Has Improved, But the Hardest Gaps Remain

Global electricity access rose from 83% in 2010 to 91% in 2020, reducing the number of people without electricity to 733 million. But progress has slowed, and if current trends continue, 670 million people could still be unserved in 2030.[32]

Clean Cooking Is Still a Major Global Challenge

By 2020, only 69% of the global population had access to clean cooking fuels and technologies. That leaves about 2.4 billion people still relying on polluting and inefficient cooking systems that increase health risks.[32]

Renewables Are Growing, But Not Fast Enough Across All Sectors

Renewables reached 17.7% of total final energy consumption in 2019. The electricity sector made the fastest gains, rising from 19.7% in 2010 to 26.2% in 2019, while progress in heating and transport remained much slower.[32]

Energy Efficiency Needs a Much Bigger Push

Global primary energy intensity improved at an average annual rate of 1.9% from 2010 to 2019. To stay aligned with the 2030 target, improvements need to average about 3.2% per year going forward.[32]

The report also notes that international public financial flows for clean energy in developing countries fell to $10.9 billion in 2019, showing that meeting Goal 7 will require much stronger policy support and far greater public and private investment.[32]
Clean energy available now

Major Clean Energy Options in Use Today

The U.S. Department of Energy identifies wind, solar, hydropower, geothermal, bioenergy, and marine energy as major renewable pathways. Nuclear energy is also widely treated as a clean electricity source because it produces carbon free electricity during operation.[3], [8], [9]

Solar

Solar Energy

Solar energy captures sunlight and converts it into electricity or heat. It is widely used in homes, businesses, and large utility scale arrays, making it one of the most visible and accessible clean-energy technologies today.[9]

Available now Distributed generation Rapidly improving
  • Current technology: crystalline silicon PV, thin film PV, utility scale solar farms, rooftop systems.[9]
  • Research/projects: DOE is advancing perovskite and tandem solar cells to increase efficiency and support future manufacturing scale up.[10], [11]
Wind

Wind Energy

Wind energy uses moving air to spin turbine blades and generate electricity. It is already a major renewable source on land and is expanding offshore, where stronger and steadier winds can increase output potential.[9]

Available now Onshore + offshore Grid-scale
  • Current technology: land based wind farms and fixed bottom offshore systems.[9]
  • Research/projects: DOE is funding floating offshore wind platforms, dynamic cables, and transmission solutions to unlock deeper water sites.[12], [13]
Water power

Hydropower and Pumped Storage

Hydropower generates electricity from moving water and remains one of the most established renewable sources in the world. Pumped storage systems also act like large grid batteries by storing energy and releasing it when electricity demand rises.[15], [16]

Available now Reliable output Supports grid stability
  • Current technology: conventional hydropower plants and pumped storage systems.[15], [16]
  • Research/projects: DOE continues work on next generation hydropower, marine energy, and storage integration.[15], [17]
Geothermal

Geothermal Energy

Geothermal energy uses heat from inside the Earth to produce electricity or provide direct heating. It is valuable because it can deliver steady energy output without depending on sunlight or wind conditions.[18], [20]

Available now Steady generation Low-emission heat source
  • Current technology: hydrothermal geothermal plants and direct use geothermal systems.[18], [20]
  • Research/projects: DOE supports enhanced geothermal systems and FORGE to expand geothermal access beyond traditional resource areas.[18], [20]
Marine

Marine Energy

Marine energy captures power from waves, tides, and ocean currents. It is still developing compared with solar and wind, but it remains an important clean energy option because oceans contain large amounts of untapped kinetic energy.[19], [21]

Emerging technology Ocean-based power Future potential
  • Current technology: pilot wave devices, tidal systems, and prototype marine converters.[19]
  • Research/projects: DOE supports marine energy materials, manufacturing, and prototype development for long term commercialization.[19], [21]
Bioenergy + hydrogen

Bioenergy and Clean Hydrogen

Bioenergy converts organic material into fuels, heat, or electricity, while clean hydrogen can store energy and support industries or transportation systems that are harder to electrify directly. Together, they extend clean energy use beyond the electric grid alone.[22], [23], [24]

Available now Industrial relevance Decarbonization tool
  • Current technology: biofuels, biogas pathways, electrolyzers, and fuel cell systems.[22], [23], [24]
  • Research/projects: DOE is scaling sustainable aviation fuels, electrolyzer supply chains, and hydrogen cost reductions.[22], [23], [25]
Nuclear

Nuclear Energy

Nuclear energy produces electricity through controlled fission reactions and stands out for its ability to deliver large amounts of carbon free power continuously. It remains especially important because it combines low operational emissions with very high reliability.[3], [4], [5], [6]

Available now Firm power High capacity factor
  • Current technology: commercial reactors, fuel fabrication, nuclear operations, grid connected baseload generation.[3], [6]
  • Research/projects: DOE’s Advanced Reactor Demonstration Program supports advanced reactor deployment and reduction.[26], [27]
Engineering perspective

The Role of Engineering in Clean Energy

Clean energy does not scale on ideas alone. It scales through design, testing, safety, manufacturing, operations, economics, and systems integration.

Design and Build

Engineers create the hardware and infrastructure: reactors, turbines, solar modules, storage systems, transmission lines, substations, and control systems.[9], [26]

Improve Efficiency and Cost

Engineering teams work on materials, manufacturing, digital controls, and system optimization to increase performance and lower lifecycle cost.[10], [11], [12], [23]

Protect Safety and Reliability

Safety engineering, risk analysis, inspection, maintenance, and regulatory compliance are essential for public trust and reliable operations, especially in nuclear systems.[3], [5], [6]

Engineering is where SDG 7 becomes concrete: the mission depends on people who can turn science into systems that actually work in the real world.
Why nuclear matters

Why Nuclear Energy Is Specifically Important

Nuclear energy stands out because it can produce large amounts of carbon-free electricity with a very energy-dense fuel source and strong reliability characteristics.[3], [4], [5], [6]

Reliable Clean Electricity

DOE describes nuclear power as carbon-free electricity that protects air quality while supporting the grid even when weather conditions reduce output from variable sources.[3]

Extremely Energy-Dense Fuel

DOE notes that uranium fuel contains tremendous energy density, which helps reduce fuel volume and support long operating cycles.[6]

Long-Term System Value

Virginia Energy highlights nuclear power as a dependable source that contributes to energy security and low-carbon generation.[4]

Advanced Nuclear Is an Active Engineering Field

DOE’s Advanced Reactor Demonstration Program supports cost-shared work with industry to reduce licensing, construction, and operational risk for advanced reactor technologies.[26], [27]

  • Advanced reactors can expand options for clean electricity, industrial heat, and long term grid resilience.[26]
  • Nuclear engineers work on reactor systems, fuel cycles, radiation applications, safety, and power production.[5]
  • The BLS lists nuclear engineering as a specialized occupation with strong technical responsibilities and a median annual wage of $127,520 in May 2024.[5]

Challenges Still Matter

Supporting nuclear energy does not mean ignoring tradeoffs. DOE also highlights challenges that include high upfront capital cost, waste management, and complex project delivery.[3]

  • That is why engineering, policy, regulation, project management, and public communication all matter.
  • For students, nuclear is a strong example of how sustainability problems require both technical depth and societal trust.
Student-centered impact

How Clean Energy Affects Communities and College Students

Clean energy influences public health, cost stability, resilience, and opportunity. That makes it directly relevant to student life, not just national policy.[2], [3], [28], [29]

Cleaner Air and Health

EPA links cleaner energy systems and pollution reduction to better air quality, lower health risks, and improved public health outcomes.[28], [29]

Campus Resilience

Reliable power affects lighting, internet access, lab work, housing, food systems, and emergency response. Energy infrastructure failures hit students fast.

Career Pipelines

DOE and national laboratories offer internships, fellowships, and student pathways in energy, engineering, research, policy, and operations.[14], [15], [16]

Action steps

How College Students Can Get Involved

You do not need to wait until graduation to contribute. Students can enter clean energy through technical, community, and policy pathways.

Join Research and Internships

  • Apply to DOE internships, fellowships, and recent graduate programs.[14], [15]
  • Explore national laboratory internships such as NREL and SULI opportunities.[16], [30]
  • Look for faculty labs in energy systems, materials, grid modeling, nuclear, or sustainability.

Lead on Campus

  • Support student chapters tied to ASME, IEEE, SWE, NSBE, ANS, ASCE, or sustainability councils.
  • Push for energy audits, solar projects, smarter building controls, and better transit options.
  • Turn course projects into real campus proposals.

Build Public Understanding

  • Use credible sources when discussing renewables, nuclear, storage, and emissions.
  • Translate technical ideas for nontechnical audiences.
  • Vote, advocate, and participate in local energy planning discussions.

About us

About Our Team

We are a team of engineering students from the University of South Florida creating a student focused website on Affordable and Clean Energy, with a special emphasis on nuclear energy as a reliable source of clean power. Our goal is to make clean energy understandable, relevant, and visually engaging for student audiences.

We recognize that the climate crisis, pollution, and biodiversity loss are closely tied to overreliance on nonrenewable energy. Because of that, students should understand not only how clean energy helps the environment, but also how it affects the economy, infrastructure, and quality of life.

Our team is committed to presenting accurate information through clear communication and strong visual design. We want this website to be a starting point for students who are trying to understand clean energy and decide how they can get involved.

  • Student-friendly explanations
  • Technical and creative teamwork
  • Strong emphasis on accessibility and reliability
  • Motivation for action through advocacy, awareness, and everyday choices
