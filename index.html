What Goal 7 stands for

What Is UN Sustainable Development Goal 7?

Goal 7 is the United Nations’ commitment to making sure everyone has access to affordable, reliable, sustainable, and modern energy. It matters because energy affects almost everything, including healthcare, education, communication, economic growth, and quality of life.[1], [2]

SDG 7 is not just about producing more electricity. It is about whether energy is actually accessible, dependable, cleaner to use, and backed by the infrastructure and partnerships needed to keep it available long term. In real life, that means expanding electricity access, increasing renewable energy use, improving efficiency, investing in clean-energy research, and strengthening infrastructure where it is still lacking.[1], [2]

For college students, SDG 7 shows up in everyday life. It affects dorm power use, transportation, internet access, utility costs, air quality, and the kinds of engineering and sustainability jobs that are growing right now.
Official UN targets

The Five SDG 7 Targets

These targets come from the official United Nations SDG 7 framework.[1]

Target 7.1

Universal Modern Energy Access

By 2030, make sure everyone has access to affordable, reliable, and modern energy services.[1]

Target 7.2

Increase Renewables

Substantially increase the share of renewable energy in the global energy mix by 2030.[1]

Target 7.3

Improve Efficiency

Double the global rate of improvement in energy efficiency by 2030.[1]

Target 7.a

Expand Research and Investment

Increase international cooperation in clean energy research, technology, and investment.[1]

Target 7.b

Build Infrastructure

Expand and upgrade energy infrastructure and technology in developing countries.[1]

UN Progress Snapshot

What the 2022 UN Goal 7 Report Highlights

The 2022 UN Goal 7 report makes one thing clear. Progress is happening, but not fast enough. More people have electricity now than before, but millions still do not. Clean cooking access is still behind, renewables need to grow faster, and energy efficiency gains are still below what is needed to hit 2030 targets.[32]

Electricity Access Has Improved, But Big Gaps Are Still There

The global electricity access increased from 83% in 2010 to 91% in 2020. That lowered the number of people without electricity to 733 million. Even so, if progress keeps moving at the current pace, about 670 million people could still be without electricity in 2030.[32]

Clean Cooking Is Still a Huge Issue

In 2020, only 69% of the global population had access to clean cooking fuels and technologies. That means about 2.4 billion people still relied on polluting and inefficient cooking systems, which can seriously affect health and quality of life.[32]

Renewables Are Growing, But the Growth Is Uneven

Renewables made up 17.7% of total final energy consumption in 2019. The biggest progress happened in electricity, where the renewable share rose from 19.7% in 2010 to 26.2% in 2019. Progress in heating and transportation was much slower.[32]

Energy Efficiency Still Needs a Major Push

Global primary energy intensity improved by an average of 1.9% per year from 2010 to 2019. To stay on track for 2030, that rate needs to be closer to 3.2% per year. In other words, the world needs to move faster.[32]

The report also notes that the amount of international public funding flows towards clean energy in developing nations dropped to $10.9 billion in 2019. This indicates that achieving Goal 7 will definitely demand more effective policies along with more investments.[32]
Clean energy available now

Major Clean Energy Options in Use Today

The U.S. Department of Energy identifies wind, solar, hydropower, geothermal, bioenergy, and marine energy as major renewable energies. Nuclear energy is also widely considered a clean source of electricity because it produces carbon free electricity during operation.[3], [8], [9]

Solar

Solar Energy

Solar energy captures sunlight and turns it into electricity or heat. It is one of the most recognizable clean energy technologies because you can see it on rooftops, campus buildings, parking structures, and large solar farms.[9]

Available now Flexible use Still improving
  • Current technology: crystalline silicon PV, thin film PV, utility scale solar farms, rooftop systems.[9]
  • Research/projects: DOE is advancing perovskite and tandem solar cells to improve efficiency and support future scale up.[10], [11]
Wind

Wind Energy

Wind energy uses moving air to spin turbine blades and generate electricity. It is already a major renewable source on land, and offshore wind is growing because ocean winds can be stronger and more consistent.[9]

Available now Onshore + offshore Large-scale power
  • Current technology: land based wind farms and fixed-bottom offshore systems.[9]
  • Research/projects: DOE is supporting floating offshore wind platforms, dynamic cables, and transmission solutions for deeper water sites.[12], [13]
Water power

Hydropower and Pumped Storage

Hydropower generates electricity from moving water and is one of the oldest and most established renewable energy sources. Pumped storage is especially useful because it helps store energy and balance the grid during high demand periods.[15], [16]

Available now Reliable output Supports the grid
  • Current technology: conventional hydropower plants and pumped storage systems.[15], [16]
  • Research/projects: DOE continues work on the next generation hydropower, marine energy, and storage integration.[15], [17]
Geothermal

Geothermal Energy

Geothermal energy uses heat from inside the Earth to produce electricity or provide heating. One of its biggest strengths is consistency, since it does not depend on weather in the same way solar and wind do.[18], [20]

Available now Steady output Low-emission heat source
  • Current technology: hydrothermal geothermal plants and direct use geothermal systems.[18], [20]
  • Research/projects: DOE supports enhanced geothermal systems and FORGE to expand geothermal access beyond traditional resource areas.[18], [20]
Marine

Marine Energy

Marine energy captures power from waves, tides, and ocean currents. It is still developing compared with solar and wind, but it has strong future potential because oceans hold a huge amount of untapped energy.[19], [21]

Emerging technology Ocean based power Long term potential
  • Current technology: pilot wave devices, tidal systems, and prototype marine converters.[19]
  • Research/projects: DOE supports marine energy materials, manufacturing, and prototype development for future commercialization.[19], [21]
Bioenergy + hydrogen

Bioenergy and Clean Hydrogen

Bioenergy converts organic materials into fuel, heat, or electricity. Clean hydrogen can store energy and help decarbonize industries and transportation systems that are harder to electrify directly. Together, they broaden what clean energy can do beyond just powering buildings.[22], [23], [24]

Available now Industrial relevance Useful beyond the grid
  • Current technology: biofuels, biogas pathways, electrolyzers, and fuel cell systems.[22], [23], [24]
  • Research/projects: DOE is scaling sustainable aviation fuels, electrolyzer supply chains, and hydrogen cost reductions.[22], [23], [25]
Nuclear

Nuclear Energy

Nuclear energy produces electricity through controlled fission reactions. It stands out because it can deliver large amounts of carbon free electricity consistently, which is a big reason it stays central in clean energy conversations.[3], [4], [5], [6]

Available now Reliable power High capacity factor
  • Current technology: commercial reactors, fuel fabrication, nuclear operations, grid connected baseload generation.[3], [6]
  • Research/projects: DOE’s Advanced Reactor Demonstration Program supports advanced reactor deployment and first-of-a-kind (FOAK) risk reduction.[26], [27]
Engineering perspective

The Role of Engineering in Clean Energy

Clean energy does not become real just because people care about it. It becomes real because engineers design it, test it, improve it, and make it work at scale.

Design and Build

Engineers create the systems behind clean energy, including reactors, turbines, solar modules, storage systems, transmission lines, substations, and control systems.[9], [26]

Improve Efficiency and Cost

Engineering teams work on materials, manufacturing, digital controls, and system optimization to improve performance and lower long term cost.[10], [11], [12], [23]

Protect Safety and Reliability

Safety engineering, risk analysis, inspection, maintenance, and regulatory compliance all matter, especially in systems like nuclear energy where reliability and trust are critical.[3], [5], [6]

This is where SDG 7 becomes real. It depends on people who can turn ideas into systems that actually work for communities.
Why nuclear matters

Why Nuclear Energy Is Specifically Important

Nuclear energy stands out because it can produce large amounts of carbon free electricity while also offering strong reliability and very energy dense fuel.[3], [4], [5], [6]

Reliable Clean Electricity

DOE describes nuclear power as a source of carbon-free electricity that can support the grid even when weather conditions reduce output from variable sources like wind and solar.[3]

Extremely Energy Dense Fuel

DOE notes that uranium fuel contains a huge amount of energy, which helps support long operating cycles and large scale power generation.[6]

Long Term System Value

Virginia Energy highlights nuclear power as a dependable source that supports energy security and low carbon electricity production.[4]

Advanced Nuclear Is an Active Engineering Field

DOE’s Advanced Reactor Demonstration Program supports work with industry to reduce licensing, construction, and operational risk for advanced reactor technologies.[26], [27]

  • Advanced reactors could expand options for clean electricity, industrial heat, and long term grid reliability.[26]
  • Nuclear engineers work on reactor systems, fuel cycles, radiation applications, safety, and power generation.[5]
  • The BLS lists nuclear engineering as a specialized occupation with strong technical responsibilities and a median annual wage of $127,520 in May 2024.[5]

The Challenges Still Matter

Supporting nuclear energy does not mean ignoring the tradeoffs. DOE also points to challenges like high upfront cost, waste management, and complex project delivery.[3]

  • That is why engineering, policy, regulation, project management, and public communication all matter.
  • For students, nuclear is a good example of how sustainability problems are both technical and social.
Student-centered impact

How Clean Energy Affects Communities and College Students

Clean energy shapes public health, cost stability, resilience, and opportunity. That makes it relevant to student life in a very real way, not just as a policy issue.[2], [3], [28], [29]

Cleaner Air and Health

EPA connects cleaner energy systems and pollution reduction to better air quality, lower health risks, and stronger public health outcomes.[28], [29]

Campus Resilience

Reliable energy affects lighting, internet access, labs, housing, food systems, and emergency response. When energy systems fail, students feel it immediately.

Career Pipelines

DOE and national laboratories offer internships, fellowships, and student pathways in engineering, research, policy, and operations.[14], [15], [16]

Action steps

How College Students Can Get Involved

You do not have to wait until graduation to be part of clean energy work. There are real ways to start now.

Join Research and Internships

  • Apply to DOE internships, fellowships, and recent graduate programs.[14], [15]
  • Look into national laboratory programs such as NREL and SULI.[16], [30]
  • Check whether faculty at your school work in energy systems, sustainability, materials, grid modeling, or nuclear topics.

Lead on Campus

  • Join student organizations tied to engineering, sustainability, or public service.
  • Support energy audits, solar initiatives, better building controls, and improved transportation options on campus.
  • Turn class projects into ideas your campus could actually use.

Build Public Understanding

  • Use strong sources when talking about renewables, nuclear, storage, and emissions.
  • Practice explaining technical ideas in a way nontechnical audiences can understand.
  • Get involved in local energy discussions, advocacy efforts, and community education.

About us

About Our Team

We are a team of engineering students from the University of South Florida creating a student focused website about affordable and clean energy, with a stronger focus on nuclear energy as a reliable source of clean power.

Our goal is to make clean energy easier to understand and more relevant to students. We want this topic to feel connected to real life, not distant or abstract.

We know climate change, pollution, and biodiversity loss are closely connected to how the world produces and uses energy. Because of that, students should understand not only how clean energy affects the environment, but also how it connects to infrastructure, the economy, and daily life.

  • Student centered explanations
  • Engineering and communication working together
  • Strong focus on accessibility and reliability
  • A goal of helping students feel informed enough to take action
